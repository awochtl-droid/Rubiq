How Often Should I
Rebalance My Portfolio?

February 24, 2026 5 min read Andreas Wochtl, CFP® · Rubiq Financial Partners

Rebalancing is one of those investment decisions that seems simple on the surface and reveals surprising complexity the moment you look closely. The question is not whether to rebalance — nearly every serious practitioner agrees you should — but how often, by how much, and under what conditions.

Why Rebalancing Matters at All

When you set a target asset allocation — say, 60% equities and 40% bonds — you are making a deliberate statement about your risk tolerance, your time horizon, and your expected return requirements. Markets don't stand still. Over time, the assets that outperform will grow to represent a larger share of your portfolio than you intended, and your actual risk exposure will drift away from your stated preferences.

A portfolio that started at 60/40 in early 2020 and was never rebalanced would have arrived at approximately 75/25 by the end of 2021, reflecting the dramatic outperformance of equities. An investor who thought they held a moderate-risk portfolio would have actually been holding something considerably more aggressive — fine during a bull market, but painful in 2022's drawdown.

Rebalancing is, in essence, a risk management discipline. It forces you to sell what has risen and buy what has fallen — a practice that is intellectually simple but emotionally difficult, which is precisely why systematic rules help.

Calendar Rebalancing: Pros and Cons

The appeal of calendar rebalancing is its simplicity. You set a date — January 1st, or the end of each quarter — and you rebalance regardless of market conditions. This removes emotion from the process entirely. The weakness is inefficiency: if markets haven't moved much, you're paying transaction costs and potentially triggering capital gains for minimal benefit. If markets have moved dramatically, you may be catching the drift weeks or months late.

Threshold Rebalancing: The Research Favorite

Most academic research on rebalancing frequency concludes that a tolerance band approach outperforms calendar rebalancing on both a risk-adjusted and tax-adjusted basis. The T. Rowe Price research group found that a ±5% tolerance band for each asset class strikes the best balance between drift control and transaction efficiency. Vanguard's 2019 analysis reached a similar conclusion, finding that the choice of specific threshold matters less than simply having and following a consistent rule.

The practical challenge is that threshold rebalancing requires ongoing monitoring — either by you or your advisor. If you don't check your portfolio regularly, you won't know when a threshold has been crossed.

A Hybrid That Works Well in Practice

For most clients, we find that a hybrid approach works best: check allocations quarterly, and rebalance if any asset class has drifted more than 5% from its target weight. This combines the discipline of a regular review cadence with the efficiency of acting only when drift is meaningful. Annual reviews catch the gradual drift; the 5% trigger catches sudden market dislocations.

"The optimal rebalancing strategy is not the one with the best backtest — it is the one you will actually follow consistently across bull markets, bear markets, and everything in between."

The Tax Dimension

In a tax-advantaged account (IRA, 401k, Roth), rebalancing is frictionless. You can sell appreciated positions without triggering capital gains and buy underweighted assets freely. Rebalance as often as your strategy calls for with no tax penalty.

In a taxable brokerage account, every sale of an appreciated position generates a taxable event. This changes the calculus meaningfully:

  • Use new contributions first. Before selling anything, direct new cash flows into underweighted asset classes. This is the most tax-efficient rebalancing method available and should always be the first step.
  • Harvest losses to offset gains. If some positions have declined in value, selling them creates losses that offset capital gains generated by selling appreciated positions. Tax-loss harvesting is a powerful tool that, done systematically, can cover a significant portion of rebalancing's tax drag.
  • Prioritize long-term capital gains. If you must sell appreciated assets, hold them for more than a year first. Long-term capital gains rates (0%, 15%, or 20% depending on income) are substantially lower than short-term rates (ordinary income).
  • Consider which accounts hold which assets. Asset location — placing tax-inefficient assets (bonds, REITs, active funds) in tax-advantaged accounts and tax-efficient assets (index ETFs, growth stocks) in taxable accounts — reduces the rebalancing tax burden over time.

How Large Portfolios Differ

For portfolios above $2M, the rebalancing conversation expands considerably. The absolute dollar amounts involved mean that each trade carries meaningful tax consequences, and the portfolio complexity — multiple account types, alternative investments, concentrated positions — requires more sophisticated coordination.

Concentrated positions in a single stock (often a founder's holding or employer equity) require particular care. The standard rebalancing heuristic of "sell what's risen" can generate enormous capital gains taxes if applied naively to a concentrated position. Strategies like exchange funds, charitable remainder trusts, or protective options strategies may be preferable to an outright sale.

Multi-account households also benefit from viewing all accounts — personal taxable, IRA, Roth, spouse's accounts, trust accounts — as a single unified portfolio. Rebalancing across the entire household while optimizing for tax location can significantly improve after-tax outcomes versus managing each account in isolation.

The Behavioral Case for Systematic Rebalancing

Perhaps the strongest argument for a rules-based rebalancing policy is not the financial one — it is the behavioral one. Markets generate powerful emotional signals. At the peak of a bull market, selling your outperforming equities and buying underperforming bonds feels deeply counterintuitive. During a crash, buying equities when they've fallen 30% while selling bonds feels terrifying.

A pre-committed rebalancing policy converts these emotional moments into mechanical execution. When your calendar says January 1st, or when your monitoring system flags a threshold breach, the decision has already been made. You are not trying to be brave in the moment — you are following a plan you made when you were calm.

Decades of behavioral finance research confirm that investors who follow systematic investment processes significantly outperform those who make discretionary decisions at market extremes. Rebalancing policy is one of the clearest expressions of that principle.

The Rubiq Approach

We review every client's portfolio on a quarterly basis, with a threshold trigger of ±5% from target weights for major asset classes. For taxable accounts, we coordinate rebalancing with tax-loss harvesting opportunities and new contribution timing. For clients with concentrated equity positions, we address rebalancing through a multi-year plan that manages the tax impact across multiple calendar years.

Beyond calendar and threshold triggers, our process incorporates a volatility overlay: we monitor the VIX as a real-time signal of market stress, using elevated readings as a prompt to evaluate whether rebalancing makes sense ahead of a scheduled review. When the VIX spikes — reflecting genuine fear and dislocated prices — that is often the most opportune moment to add to underweight positions, even if a portfolio hasn't yet breached its drift threshold. Discipline matters, but so does context. What we've found, across years of working with entrepreneurs and investors, is that evaluating rebalancing decisions against both drift and market conditions — not just a calendar — leads to better outcomes than any single-trigger rule alone.
