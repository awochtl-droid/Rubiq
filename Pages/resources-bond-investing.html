Bond Investing: From Treasuries
to Private Credit

March 26, 2026 12 min read Andreas Wochtl, CFP® · Rubiq Financial Partners

Fixed income is not a single asset class — it is a spectrum. From the near-zero credit risk of U.S. Treasuries to the illiquidity premiums of private credit and hard money lending, each segment carries a distinct risk-return profile. Understanding those tradeoffs is the first step toward building a fixed income allocation that actually serves your goals.

This guide walks through seven segments of the bond market, from the most conservative to the most aggressive. For each, we cover how it works, what it pays, and what can go wrong. No segment is universally "good" or "bad" — the right mix depends on your income needs, liquidity requirements, risk tolerance, and tax situation.

1. U.S. Treasury Securities

Yield: ~4.2–4.6% (as of early 2026)
Credit Risk: Lowest

U.S. Treasury securities — bills, notes, and bonds — are backed by the full faith and credit of the U.S. government. They are widely considered the global benchmark for "risk-free" fixed income. Treasuries are issued by the U.S. Department of the Treasury in maturities ranging from 4 weeks to 30 years, and their interest is exempt from state and local income tax.

Strengths

  • No credit risk — backed by U.S. government
  • Highly liquid — deepest bond market in the world
  • State and local tax exempt
  • Excellent portfolio diversifier during equity selloffs
  • Available in inflation-protected form (TIPS)

Risks & Limitations

  • Interest rate risk — prices fall when rates rise (duration risk)
  • Lower yields than other fixed income sectors
  • Inflation can erode real returns if not using TIPS
  • Opportunity cost relative to higher-yielding alternatives

Treasury securities remain the foundation of most fixed income portfolios. According to SIFMA, the U.S. Treasury market exceeded $26 trillion in outstanding securities as of 2025, making it the largest and most liquid bond market in the world. That liquidity means investors can buy or sell virtually any amount with minimal price impact — a quality that becomes invaluable during periods of market stress.

2. Agency Debt & Mortgage-Backed Securities

Yield: ~4.5–5.5%
Credit Risk: Very Low

Agency bonds are issued by government-sponsored enterprises (GSEs) such as Fannie Mae, Freddie Mac, and Ginnie Mae, as well as by Federal Home Loan Banks and other federal agencies. Ginnie Mae securities carry the explicit full faith and credit guarantee of the U.S. government; Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac carry an implicit (though widely assumed) government backstop.

Strengths

  • Near-sovereign credit quality with a yield premium over Treasuries
  • Ginnie Mae MBS carry explicit U.S. government guarantee
  • Deep, liquid secondary market
  • Broad range of maturities and structures
  • Monthly income from mortgage-backed pass-through securities

Risks & Limitations

  • Prepayment risk — borrowers refinance when rates fall, returning principal early
  • Extension risk — when rates rise, prepayments slow and duration extends
  • Fannie/Freddie carry implicit, not explicit, guarantee
  • More complex cash flow patterns than plain-vanilla bonds

Agency MBS are a staple of institutional fixed income portfolios and are among the most actively traded securities in the world. The Federal Reserve has historically been one of the largest holders and buyers of agency MBS, which underscores the market’s depth. For investors, the yield pickup over Treasuries — typically 50–100 basis points — compensates for the added complexity of prepayment modeling.

3. Investment-Grade Corporate Bonds

Yield: ~5.0–6.0%
Credit Risk: Low to Moderate

Investment-grade (IG) corporate bonds are issued by companies rated BBB− or higher by S&P Global Ratings (or Baa3 by Moody’s). These are typically large, financially stable corporations — think Apple, Johnson & Johnson, or JPMorgan Chase. Historical annual default rates for investment-grade issuers have averaged approximately 0.1%, making credit losses rare.

Strengths

  • Yield premium of ~100–150 bps over Treasuries
  • Low historical default rates (~0.1% per year for IG)
  • Liquid secondary market, especially for benchmark issues
  • Wide issuer diversification available
  • Transparent pricing and credit research readily available

Risks & Limitations

  • Credit spread widening during recessions reduces price
  • Interest rate sensitivity (duration risk)
  • BBB-rated bonds (“fallen angel” risk) can be downgraded to high yield
  • Concentrated issuance in financials, tech, and healthcare
  • Taxable at federal, state, and local levels

The investment-grade corporate market is the workhorse of many taxable fixed income portfolios. According to the ICE BofA U.S. Corporate Index, the market represents over $7 trillion in outstanding debt. The key consideration for investors is the balance between credit spread compensation and interest rate sensitivity — longer-duration IG bonds can experience meaningful price declines when rates rise, even if no credit event occurs.

“In fixed income, the goal is not to find the highest yield — it is to find the right yield for the right risk at the right place in the portfolio. Every basis point of additional return comes with a tradeoff.”

4. High-Yield Corporate Bonds

Yield: ~7.0–9.0%
Credit Risk: High

High-yield bonds — sometimes called “junk bonds” — are issued by companies rated below BBB− by S&P or Ba1 by Moody’s. These issuers carry more leverage, weaker cash flow coverage, or less established business models. In exchange, they offer significantly higher yields. According to Moody’s, the historical average annual default rate for speculative-grade issuers has been approximately 4.1% since 1983.

Strengths

  • Significantly higher yields (200–500+ bps above IG)
  • Lower interest rate sensitivity (shorter duration profile)
  • Equity-like return potential in recovery scenarios
  • Diversification benefit vs. pure equity portfolios
  • Liquid market with robust ETF and mutual fund access

Risks & Limitations

  • Meaningful default risk (3–5% annual average, higher in recessions)
  • Recovery rates average ~40% of face value in default (Moody’s)
  • High correlation with equities — drops during risk-off periods
  • Credit spread widening can cause sharp drawdowns
  • Concentrated sectors: energy, telecom, healthcare

High yield occupies an important middle ground between investment-grade bonds and equities. Research from J.P. Morgan has shown that high-yield bonds tend to behave more like equities during market stress — credit spreads widen sharply when equity markets sell off — which limits their diversification value precisely when it is most needed. For investors with the risk tolerance and time horizon to absorb periodic drawdowns, high yield can serve as an income-enhancing allocation, but it should not be confused with a true defensive asset.

5. Collateralized Loan Obligations (CLOs)

Yield: ~5.5–12%+ (varies by tranche)
Credit Risk: Varies by Tranche

CLOs are structured vehicles that pool together 150–300 leveraged loans (typically to non-investment-grade borrowers) and issue tranches of debt against that pool. The senior-most tranche (AAA) gets paid first and absorbs losses last; the equity tranche absorbs first losses but captures the excess spread. According to S&P Global Ratings, AAA-rated CLO tranches have experienced zero defaults since the modern CLO structure was introduced in the 1990s.

Strengths

  • Floating-rate — natural hedge against rising interest rates
  • Senior tranches (AAA–A) have strong credit performance history
  • Built-in diversification across 150–300 underlying loans
  • Structural protections (overcollateralization, interest diversion tests)
  • Attractive spread vs. similarly rated corporate bonds

Risks & Limitations

  • Structural complexity — difficult for non-specialists to evaluate
  • Lower tranches (BB, B, equity) carry meaningful loss risk
  • Underlying collateral is non-investment-grade leveraged loans
  • Liquidity can be limited, especially for mezzanine and equity tranches
  • Mark-to-market volatility can exceed fundamental credit risk
  • Manager quality varies significantly

CLOs have gained popularity among institutional investors and increasingly among high-net-worth investors seeking floating-rate income. It is essential to understand that the risk profile of a CLO investment depends almost entirely on which tranche you own. An investor in AAA CLOs is taking on very different risk than an investor in CLO equity. The LSTA (Loan Syndications and Trading Association) provides market data and educational resources on the leveraged loan market that underlies CLO collateral pools.

6. Private Credit & Direct Lending

Yield: ~9–12%+ (direct lending)
Credit Risk: Moderate to High

Private credit refers to non-bank lending where investors provide debt financing directly to businesses outside of public bond markets. The asset class has grown to approximately $1.7 trillion in AUM (Preqin, 2024) and is projected to reach $2.8 trillion by 2028. Senior secured direct loans typically yield SOFR + 500–700 basis points, translating to all-in yields of 10–12%.

Strengths

  • Significant yield premium over public fixed income
  • Floating-rate — income rises with interest rates
  • Senior secured with negotiated covenants
  • Lower mark-to-market volatility (quarterly valuations)
  • Direct relationship with borrower — control over terms

Risks & Limitations

  • Illiquid — 5–10 year lockups with limited secondary market
  • Valuation opacity (manager-marked, not market-priced)
  • Wide manager dispersion — selection risk is substantial
  • Concentrated borrower exposure (30–50 names per fund)
  • Not stress-tested through a severe, prolonged recession at current scale
  • Higher minimums ($250K–$1M+)

For a deeper exploration of private credit strategies, including mezzanine debt, distressed debt, and real estate debt, see our full article on private credit and alternatives. The critical point for investors is that private credit’s lower reported volatility is partly an artifact of infrequent, manager-determined valuations — it does not mean the underlying risk is lower. As the Federal Reserve’s Financial Stability Report has noted, the rapid growth of private credit warrants attention to systemic risk implications.

7. Hard Money & Specialty Lending

Yield: ~10–20%+
Credit Risk: High to Very High

Hard money lending sits at the highest-yield end of the fixed income spectrum. These are short-to-medium-term loans — typically 6 to 36 months — made to borrowers who cannot access traditional bank financing due to timing, credit profile, or the nature of the underlying project. Lenders can range from institutional funds to private individuals, and the lending use cases are varied: bridge financing for real estate acquisitions, construction loans, litigation finance, inventory lending, and other specialty lending profiles. The common thread is that the borrower is paying a substantial premium for speed, flexibility, or access.

Strengths

  • Highest yields in the fixed income spectrum (10–20%+)
  • Often secured by real property, legal judgments, or business assets
  • Short hold periods (6–36 months) limit duration exposure
  • Floating-rate structure in most cases
  • Low correlation with public bond and equity markets
  • Diverse use cases — real estate, litigation, construction, inventory

Risks & Limitations

  • High default risk — borrowers are often distressed or speculative
  • Collateral value can deteriorate (real estate declines, failed projects)
  • Highly illiquid — no secondary market for individual loans
  • Sponsor and originator selection is the single most important factor
  • Foreclosure and workout processes are costly, slow, and uncertain
  • Concentration risk if investing in individual loans vs. a diversified fund
  • Regulatory complexity varies by state and loan type
  • Litigation finance outcomes are binary — win or lose

Hard money lending can generate compelling returns, but it is unforgiving of poor underwriting. Whether the loan is a bridge to permanent real estate financing, a construction draw facility, or pre-settlement litigation funding, the sponsor’s ability to evaluate the borrower, structure appropriate protections, and manage workouts when loans go sideways is what separates consistent returns from catastrophic losses. Investors should scrutinize loan-to-value ratios (typically 55–70% for first-lien positions), the borrower’s track record, and the clearly defined exit strategy before committing capital.

For individual investors, accessing this space through institutional-quality funds with proper diversification is far preferable to making direct loans. The SEC’s guidance on private placements provides important context for evaluating these opportunities.

“The most common mistake in fixed income is chasing yield without understanding the risk you are taking to get it. Every segment has a role — the discipline is in knowing which role each one plays in your portfolio.”

Sponsor Selection Is Everything

If there is one theme that runs through every segment beyond Treasuries and agency debt, it is this: the quality of the sponsor or manager is the single most important determinant of investment outcomes. This is true for investment-grade bond fund managers, but the stakes escalate dramatically as you move into CLOs, private credit, and hard money lending. The dispersion between top-quartile and bottom-quartile managers in private fixed income is far wider than in public markets. A well-underwritten private credit fund can deliver consistent, equity-like returns with senior-secured protection. A poorly managed one can suffer defaults, impaired collateral, and permanent capital loss.

Sponsor due diligence is not optional — it is the investment decision. Track record through a full credit cycle, underwriting discipline, workout capability, fee transparency, and alignment of interests (co-investment, hurdle rates) all matter. The Role of an Advisor

Building a fixed income allocation is not simply about picking the highest-yielding category. It requires balancing income needs with liquidity requirements, managing interest rate exposure alongside credit risk, and accounting for tax efficiency. A concentrated position in any single segment — even Treasuries — introduces risks that diversification across the spectrum can mitigate.

At Rubiq, we construct fixed income portfolios across the risk spectrum, calibrated to each client’s specific situation. Whether that means a conservative Treasury and agency allocation for someone in early retirement, or a more aggressive mix of CLOs, private credit, and hard money lending for a client with a long time horizon and ample liquidity elsewhere, the structure is driven by the plan — not by reaching for yield. And across every allocation, rigorous sponsor selection — supported by institutional-grade research from partners like CAIS and iCapital — is what separates thoughtful portfolio construction from reckless yield-chasing.
